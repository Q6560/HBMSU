Master these formulas to unlock medical terminology

Master Thesis #1: HYPER vs HYPO

HYPER = HIGH, EXCESSIVE, ABOVE | HYPO = LOW, DEFICIENT, BELOW

Example: Hypertension (high BP >140/90) vs Hypotension (low BP <90/60)

Master Thesis #2: BRADY vs TACHY

BRADY = SLOW | TACHY = FAST, RAPID

Example: Bradycardia (heart rate <60 bpm) vs Tachycardia (heart rate >100 bpm)

Master Thesis #3: ECTOMY vs OTOMY

ECTOMY = SURGICAL REMOVAL (complete) | OTOMY = SURGICAL INCISION (cutting into)

Example: Cholecystectomy (remove gallbladder) vs Tracheotomy (cut into trachea)

Master Thesis #4: ILEUM vs ILIUM

ILEUM = Part of small intestine | ILIUM = Part of hip bone

Example: Ileitis (inflammation of small intestine) vs Iliac artery (artery near hip bone)

Word Decoding Formula: PREFIX + ROOT + SUFFIX = MEANING

Example: Gastroenteritis = Gastr(stomach) + enter(intestine) + itis(inflammation) = Stomach/intestine inflammation

Example: Hypoglycemia = Hypo(low) + glyc(sugar) + emia(blood condition) = Low blood sugar

Clinical Scenario: Describing a Wound

A patient has a deep cut on the front of their lower leg, just above the ankle. This precise language ensures everyone on the medical team knows the exact location.

Clinical Scenario: Acute Myocardial Infarction (MI)

A 65-year-old male presents to the ER with severe substernal chest pain radiating to his left arm, shortness of breath (dyspnea), and diaphoresis (sweating). An ECG shows ST-segment elevation in the anterior leads, and blood tests reveal elevated troponin levels (indicating myocardial necrosis). The diagnosis is an acute anterior **Myocardial Infarction (MI)**. The patient is immediately given aspirin and oxygen, then rushed to the cardiac catheterization lab for an emergency **coronary angiogram** to visualize the blockage. A **percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI)** with **angioplasty** and stent placement is performed to restore blood flow to the affected coronary artery, preventing further myocardial damage.

Clinical Scenario: Acute Cholecystitis

A 50-year-old female with a history of obesity presents to the ER with severe right upper quadrant (RUQ) abdominal pain that started after eating a fatty meal. Physical examination reveals Murphy's sign (pain with palpation of the RUQ during inspiration). An ultrasound reveals **gallstones** (cholelithiasis) and a thickened gallbladder wall. The diagnosis is **Cholecystitis** (chole/cyst/itis = bile/bladder/inflammation). Laboratory tests show elevated white blood cell count and liver enzymes. The patient is started on IV fluids, antibiotics, and pain management. The recommended treatment is a laparoscopic **Cholecystectomy** (chole/cyst/ectomy = bile/bladder/removal) to surgically remove the inflamed gallbladder and prevent future episodes.

Clinical Scenario: Fungal Nail Infection

A 60-year-old patient complains of a thickened, yellow, and brittle toenail that has been worsening over 6 months. The patient also reports recent nail discoloration and separation from the nail bed. A sample is taken for a KOH prep (potassium hydroxide preparation) and fungal culture, which confirms a dermatophyte infection. The diagnosis is **Onychomycosis** (onycho/myc/osis = nail/fungus/abnormal condition). Treatment options include topical antifungal agents (terbinafine, amorolfine) applied directly to the nail, or oral antifungal medications (terbinafine, itraconazole) for systemic treatment. In severe cases, the nail may need to be surgically removed to allow topical antifungals to penetrate the nail bed.

Clinical Scenario: Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)

A 28-year-old type 1 diabetic patient is brought to the ER with confusion, rapid breathing (Kussmaul respirations), fruity-smelling breath, and severe abdominal pain. Blood tests show a glucose level of 450 mg/dL, arterial pH of 7.1 (severe acidosis), and elevated ketone bodies. This is a classic presentation of **Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)**, a life-threatening emergency. The underlying cause is **Hypoglycemia** (hypo- = low, glyc/o = sugar, -emia = blood condition) - wait, actually this is **Hyperglycemia** (hyper- = high, glyc/o = sugar, -emia = blood condition) with metabolic acidosis. The patient is treated with intravenous insulin, aggressive fluid resuscitation, and electrolyte replacement to correct the acidosis and bring blood glucose under control.

Clinical Scenario: Acute Ischemic Stroke

A 72-year-old patient with hypertension presents to the ER with sudden onset of facial drooping, arm weakness, and speech difficulty (dysphasia). The patient's last known normal time was 2 hours ago. A stat CT head shows no hemorrhage. An MRI with diffusion imaging confirms an acute ischemic stroke in the middle cerebral artery (MCA) territory. The diagnosis is an acute **Cerebrovascular Accident (CVA)** or **Stroke** (ischemic type). The patient is a candidate for **thrombolysis** with tissue plasminogen activator (tPA) to dissolve the clot and restore blood flow. Time is critical - "time is brain" - as every minute of ischemia results in neuronal death.

Clinical Scenario: Community-Acquired Pneumonia

A 55-year-old patient with COPD presents with cough, fever, and dyspnea (difficulty breathing). Chest X-ray shows a left lower lobe infiltrate. Sputum culture is pending. The diagnosis is **Pneumonia** (pneum/o = lung, -ia = condition). The patient is started on broad-spectrum antibiotics (covering common respiratory pathogens like Streptococcus pneumoniae and Haemophilus influenzae) and supplemental oxygen to maintain adequate oxygenation. The condition **Hypoxemia** (hypo- = low, ox = oxygen, -emia = blood condition) is corrected with oxygen therapy.

Clinical Scenario: Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)

A 70-year-old patient with sepsis from pneumonia presents with decreased urine output (oliguria), elevated serum creatinine (2.8 mg/dL, baseline 1.0), and elevated BUN (45 mg/dL). The diagnosis is **Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)**, formerly called acute renal failure. The underlying cause is sepsis-induced hypotension leading to **acute tubular necrosis (ATN)**. The patient requires aggressive fluid resuscitation, antibiotics for the underlying infection, and close monitoring of electrolytes. If kidney function does not recover, the patient may require **hemodialysis** (hemo = blood, dialysis = separation) to remove excess fluid and waste products.

Clinical Scenario: Osteoarthritis of the Knee

A 68-year-old patient with a history of knee injury presents with chronic knee pain, stiffness, and swelling. Physical examination reveals crepitus (grinding sensation) and limited range of motion. X-rays show joint space narrowing and osteophyte (bone spur) formation. The diagnosis is **Osteoarthritis (OA)** of the knee (oste/o = bone, arthr = joint, itis = inflammation). This is a **degenerative joint disease** characterized by loss of articular cartilage. Treatment includes weight loss, physical therapy, anti-inflammatory medications (NSAIDs), and intra-articular steroid injections. In severe cases, **total knee arthroplasty (TKA)** (arthro = joint, plasty = surgical repair) may be considered to replace the damaged joint with a prosthetic.
