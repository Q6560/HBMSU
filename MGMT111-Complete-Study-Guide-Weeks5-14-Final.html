  • Use networks of contacts more than formal authority.
  • Talents include innovation, general management skills, business know-how, and networks.

üìñ Week 7: Organizing for Success

1Ô∏è‚É£ Designing Organizational Structure
Organizational structure defines the scope of acceptable behavior within an organization, its lines of authority and accountability, and to some extent, the organization's relationship with its external environment.

Designing organizational structure involves the process of organizing, arranging, and structuring the work to achieve an organization's goals (the second management function).

  • Controls how information movements between levels within the company.
  • The formal arrangement of jobs within an organization.
2Ô∏è‚É£ Organizing Process
  1. Divide the work to be done into specific jobs and departments.
  2. Assign tasks and responsibilities related to individual jobs.
  3. Manages different organizational tasks.
  4. Groups jobs into units.
  5. Establish relationships among individuals, groups, and departments.
  6. Establishes formal lines of authority.
  7. Allocates and arranges organizational resources.

Unsuccessful organizational design/A poorly designed organization led to:

  • Confusion within roles
  • A lack of coordination among functions
  • Difficulted to share the ideas
  • Slow decision-making
  • Bring managers unnecessary stress, and conflict
3Ô∏è‚É£ Organizational Design

Organizational Design is a process involving decisions about six key elements:

  1. Work specialization
  2. Departmentalization
  3. Hierarchy of authority/Chain of Command
  4. Span of control
  5. Centralization and decentralization
  6. Formalization

Each element affects how workers involve with each other, management, and their jobs to achieve the employer's goals.

4Ô∏è‚É£ Work Specialization
The degree to which tasks in the organization are divided into separate jobs.
  • Lets managers break complex tasks into smaller, more specific tasks that individual employees can complete.
  • Each worker is trained on how best to perform a specific task. Over time, that worker becomes effective.
  • Ensures work is running smoothly and quickly.

But: Over specialization can result in weakness, stress, poor quality, nonattendance, and higher turnover.

Examples: KFC: (1) Customer Service team, (2) Food Service Team. Google: (1) Engineering, (2) Design, (3) Sales Operations.

5Ô∏è‚É£ Departmentalization
Departmentalization refers to the process of grouping jobs together in order to coordinate common activities and tasks.

The most common way is division into business functions: purchasing, manufacturing, sales, accounting, etc.

6Ô∏è‚É£ Hierarchy of Authority / Chain of Command
  • Lets each member have a strong understanding of whom they report to and who makes up their team.
  • Ensures every task, job position, and department has one person taking responsibility for performance.

Authority ‚Äì Responsibility: Allocate power and responsibilities, keep employees aware of company news, create system for sharing knowledge. Ensures each employee is responsible for their work but also has senior leader to offer support, encouragement, and motivation.

7Ô∏è‚É£ Span of Control
The number of employees who can be effectively and efficiently supervised by one manager.

The width of the span is affected by:

  1. Skills and abilities of the manager (1-4 assistants/ 1-10 assistants)
  2. Employee characteristics (new/experts)
  3. Features of the work being done (call center 50/ 2 Ph.D. learners)
    • Similarity and difficulty of tasks
    • Standardization of tasks
  4. Strength of the organization's culture (collaboration/ delegation)

Structures with large spans are WIDE. Those with smaller spans are NARROW.

8Ô∏è‚É£ Tall vs. Flat Structures

Organizations can be described in terms of width and height. Companies with many levels of management (hierarchy) are tall and those with fewer levels are flat.

  • Structures with smaller spans of control are Narrow Span Of Control.
  • Structures with large spans of control are Wide Span Of Control.

üîü Centralization

Who makes the decisions in an organization?

The degree to which decision-making is concentrated at upper levels in the organization, and lower-level employees simply carry out those orders.
  • If decision-making power is concentrated at a single point, the structure is centralized.
  • If decision-making power is spread out, the structure is decentralized.

Examples: McDonald/Airlines (centralized) - Subway (decentralized)

1Ô∏è‚É£2Ô∏è‚É£ Formalization
  • Formalization is the extent to which an organization's policies, procedures, job descriptions, and rules are written.
  • These structures control employee behavior using written rules.
  • One benefit: makes employee behavior more consistent. Whenever a problem arises, employees know to turn to a handbook or procedure.
  • Employees respond to problems in similar way across organization; leads to stability of behavior.
1Ô∏è‚É£3Ô∏è‚É£ Current-Contemporary Designs

The modern organizational structure aims to make companies more active than the traditional hierarchical model.

Difference between traditional and modern:

  • Reduce management levels to increase sharing of information, personnel, and skills across departments.
  • Removes departmental borders between employees and has them work on projects together.

Types: Matrix Structure, Committee Structure, Team Design, A learning organization

1Ô∏è‚É£4Ô∏è‚É£ Current Designs Explained

Matrix Structure: The project management approach, sometimes used with traditional line-and-staff structure.

Team Design: Groups of employees are formed from many functional areas to solve problems.

Committee Structure: Authority and responsibility held by a group rather than individual. Committees typically part of larger line-and-staff organization. Often role is only suggested, but sometimes has power to make and implement decisions.

A learning organization: Any organization that selects personal and professional growth through knowledge transfer. Encourages learning as part of fundamental culture and overall vision for long-term success.

üìñ Week 8: Managing Human Resources

1Ô∏è‚É£ Strategic Human Resources Management
Human Resources Management (HRM): System of organizational activities to attract, develop, and motivate an effective and qualified employees. Also known as talent, human capital, or personnel management.

Competitive advantage: Strategic Impact of HR

  1. People create value. People can add value by helping to reduce costs and provide something unique to customers through total quality initiatives and continuous improvement.
  2. Talent is unique/rare. Top companies invest in recruiting and training the best employees to gain an advantage over competitors.
  3. Well-select/chosen, motivated people are difficult to replicate/copy.
  4. People can be organized for success. Competitive advantage comes when companies quickly align people's talents to work effectively on new assignments.
2Ô∏è‚É£ Role of the HR Function

The functions of HRM are mainly planning, directing, managing, and organizing, including Human resource management in an organization.

The role of the HR function can be divided into strategic and operational:

  • Strategic: The HR department's role and responsibilities develop to become more strategic. The function of HRM is to improve the value of leadership potential, top talent, employee retention rate, company goals, and the long-term success of the company.
  • Operational: Responsible for managing employee information, time management, finances, and setting up organizational strategies. The effectiveness of these human resource strategies is the responsibility of the HR department, which should be a part of every company's decision-making process.

  2. Design the program.

8Ô∏è‚É£ Performance Appraisal
Performance appraisal (PA) is an assessment of an employee's job performance.

PA has three basic functions:

  1. To provide feedback to each person on his or her performance.
  2. To help for modifying or changing behavior toward more effective working behaviors.
  3. To provide data to managers with which they may needs for future job assignments.

Done well, it can help people improve their performance, pay, and chances for promotion; raise communication between managers and employees; and increase the employees and the organization's effectiveness.

Done poorly, it can reduce motivation, performance, and even put the organization at risk of legal action.

9Ô∏è‚É£ Reward Systems

Many incentive systems have been developed to motivate employees to be more productive. The most common are:

  1. Individual incentive plans: Compare a worker's performance based on an objective standard, with pay linked to the employee's performance.
  2. Group incentive plans: Based on group performance and aim to give employees a feeling of participation and ownership in the organization's performance. Example: Profit-sharing plans.
  3. Merit pay: Promotions, salary and bonuses are all types of merit pay, which employers can offer as a thanks for who exceed expectations. Merit pay is a permanent increase to an employee's salary while incentive pay is temporary.
üîü Rewards vs. Recognition

While the two terms look like they have the same meaning, rewards and recognition are totally different.

  • Rewards refer to programs that measure performance and aim to motivate employees at an individual or group level. Rewards are always tangible and linked to goals. For example, rewards typically come in the form of cash, travel, gift cards.
  • Recognition is often intangible in nature. You can give recognition without giving a reward. For example, thank-you note or email to express appreciation for your employees and hard works. Public acknowledgment during a team meeting for a colleague's contributions.

You should never give a reward without giving recognition.

Providing recognition together with rewards not only acknowledges the efforts and achievements of employees but also promotes satisfaction, and a positive workplace culture.

üìñ Week 9: Diversity and Inclusion

1Ô∏è‚É£ Introduction: Diversity and Inclusion

The concept of managing diversity and inclusion in organizations has its roots in Equal Employment Opportunity (EEO).

EEO: It's the freedom from unfair treatment based on gender, color, religion, national origin, disability, and age, using their differences to the organization's advantage.

What is EEO? The basic EEO definition (or Equal Employment Opportunity) is the idea that everyone should be treated fairly when they're considered for many employment decisions (including promotion, termination, and compensation).

Example: Anyone in the company should have the same chances of promotion if they're right for the job.

2Ô∏è‚É£ Difference Between Diversity & Inclusion

Diversity and inclusion are two interconnected concepts‚Äîbut they are far from interchangeable.

  • Diversity is the representation of different people in an organization.
  • Inclusion is ensuring that everyone has an equal opportunity to contribute to and effect every part and level of a workplace.
3Ô∏è‚É£ Why Diversity & Inclusion Important?

The potential advantage of diversity and inclusion initiatives:

  1. Ability to attract and retain motivated employees.
  2. Understand different markets.
  3. Apply creativity and innovation in problem-solving.
  4. Enhance flexibility.
  5. Can contribute to organizational performance.
4Ô∏è‚É£ Diversity at the Workplace Principles
  1. Make sure at least one woman is among the shortlisted candidates.
  2. Having at least one woman in the most senior roles.
  3. Not asking about family condition/state or pregnancy (we clearly state the requirements in the job posting).
  4. Always keep an eye on diversity in internal and external recruitment.
  5. Observing equal pay for equal work among males and females.
  6. Motherhood leave does not affect annual leave or pay increases.
  7. Flexibility for parents.

Inclusion is about people achieving "social acceptance" by positively interacting with others and being valued for who they are.

As such, it must be "internally motivated" and trust that all people have value and the right to belong.

üìñ Week 10: Leadership

1Ô∏è‚É£ What Is Leadership?
  • Leadership is the art of motivating a group of people to act toward achieving a common objective.
  • Organizations refer to upper-level personnel in their management structures as leadership.
  • To be an effective business leader, you must hold qualities that extend beyond management duties.
  • Leadership skills can be learned, and leaders may develop.
2Ô∏è‚É£ Leadership vs. Management

Leadership and management are not the same. Leadership requires qualities that extend beyond management duties. Both leaders and managers must manage the resources available to them, but true leadership requires more.

Leadership Manager
Must inspire/motivate followers May or may not inspire/motivate those under them
Focuses on innovation Focuses on control
Maybe unconcerned with maintaining current structures Work within current organization structures
Effectively arranging important change Structuring the organization, recruitment it with skilled people, and observing results
  • Sometimes, effective managers can be true leaders.
  • It's very difficult to be both a great manager and a great leader because the skills required differ greatly.
3Ô∏è‚É£ The Best Leaders Behaviors

5 key behaviors of the best leaders (Kouzes and Posner):

  1. Inspire a shared vision. They appeal to people's values and motivate them to care about an important mission.
  2. Challenge the process. They challenge beliefs and practices, and they create change.
  3. Enable others to act. They give people access to information and give them the power to perform to their full potential.
  4. Model the way. They don't just tell people what to do; they are as a role model for others.
  5. Encourage the heart. They show appreciation, provide rewards, and use several approaches to motivate people in positive ways.
4Ô∏è‚É£ Traditional Approaches
  1. Trait Approach
  2. Behavioral Approach
  3. Situational Approach
5Ô∏è‚É£ Leaders Trait Approach

The trait theory of leadership suggests that certain inborn qualities and characteristics make someone a leader. The trait approach focuses only on the leader itself and raises the question of whether leaders are born or made.

Measuring traits (behavior, thought, emotion):

  1. Self-confidence: Belief in oneself, ideas, and ability.
  2. Thinking ability: Strong analytical abilities, and conceptually skilled.
  3. Honesty and integrity: trustworthy, reliable, and open.
  4. Emotional stability: well, adjusted, does not suffer from psychological illnesses.
  5. Achievement effort: High level of effort, high levels of motivation, energy, and initiative.
  6. Leadership motivation: Lead others to reach shared goals.
  7. Others: charisma, creativity, and flexibility.
  8. Knowledge of business: Knowledge of industry and other technical matters.
6Ô∏è‚É£ Leaders Behavioral Approach
  • The behavioral approach focuses on how leaders behave in the practice of their responsibilities.
  • Behavioral leadership theories focus heavily on a leader's actions‚Äîthis theory suggests that the best leadership success is viewing how a leader acts rather than their natural attributes.
  • Actions like making decisions rather than qualities are the focal points.
  • While Behavioral leadership theory suggests that anyone can become a leader, it doesn't directly suggest how to behave in certain situations.
7Ô∏è‚É£ Behavioral Approach: Two Categories

1. Task-oriented leaders: Puts more effort into seeing the systems and structure that let their team do their work.

Task-oriented leaders show the following behavior:

  1. Initiating
  2. Organizing
  3. Clarifying
  4. Information gathering

2. People-oriented leaders: Puts more effort into seeing the human needs of their team and motivating them through that.

People-oriented leaders show the following behavior:

  1. Encouraging
  2. Observing
  3. Listening
  4. Coaching and Advising

1Ô∏è‚É£ Introduction: Motivation
Motivation is one of the motivating forces behind human behavior. It encourages competition and builds social connections. Its absence may contribute to health issues such as depression.

Managers must motivate people to:

  • Join the organization.
  • Remain in the organization.
  • Come to work regularly.

3Ô∏è‚É£ Goal-Setting Theory
Goal-Setting Theory: A motivation theory states that people have goals that direct their thoughts and behaviors toward a specific end.

Well-planned goals are highly motivating. The most powerful goals are important; objectives that align with people's "higher" values add extra motivating power.

4Ô∏è‚É£ Stretch Goals
Stretch Goals: Objectives that encourage a company to complete a challenging task. A high-effort and high-risk goal intentionally set above normal standards.

Example: currently sell 1,000 units per month/ increasing to 2,000.

Stretch goals are not expected to be achieved one hundred percent. They are set to motivate growth and enhance satisfaction in teams. When achieved, it's recognized as high performance. When missed, it should not be marked as low performance.

Benefits:

  • Improvement Motivation.
  • Drive innovation & enhance development.
  • Encourage collaboration.
  • Improve workplace well-being.
  • Reduce turnover.
  • Develop company culture.
5Ô∏è‚É£ Types of Stretch Goals
  1. Vertical stretch goals: take current goals that an individual or team is already making development on and encourage them to aim higher by an extension of the goal. These stretch objectives typically work with projects that are already started. Usually, requires your current resources only.
  2. Horizontal stretch goals are all about extending the scope of your goals. These goals encourage individuals and teams to look at their goals and productivity in new ways, expanding to achieving greater growth. It often requires additional resources.
6Ô∏è‚É£ Reinforcing/Supporting Performance

Law of Effect: A law established by Edward Thorndike in 1911 states that behaviors followed by positive outcomes are more likely to be repeated.

  • Reinforcers/Motivators are positive values that motivate behavior.
  • Reinforcers/Motivators are tangible and intangible objects or actions that are used to increase the rate of certain behaviors.
7Ô∏è‚É£ Four Key Consequences

Four key consequences of behavior either encourage or discourage people's behavior:

  1. Positive reinforcement: Applying the same results by repeating the behavior leads to these results.
  2. Negative reinforcement: Removing an unattractive result.
  3. Punishment: the negative results of a situation, which reduces the possibility of a behavior being repeated in the future.
  4. Elimination/Extinction: Removing the support to prevent a behavior from being reinforced.
8Ô∏è‚É£ Be Careful What You Reinforce

Reinforcement:

  • Sometimes, organizations, and managers reinforce the wrong behaviors.
  • To use reinforcement effectively, managers must identify which kinds of behaviors they reinforce and which they discourage.
  • Don't punish people who deliver bad news to their bosses; instead, appreciate them.
  • Innovative managers use motivators effectively and non-cash rewards.

Should You Punish Mistakes?

Punishment is sometimes appropriate. When people break up the law, ethical standards, important safety rules, or standards of interpersonal treatment.

Managers who overuse punishment or use it inappropriately create an environment of panic in the workplace. The key is how to think about and handle mistakes.

9Ô∏è‚É£ Feedback Is Essential Reinforcement

Most managers don't provide enough useful feedback.

Feedback can be offered in many ways:

  • Customer comments.
  • Performance appraisals.
  • Regular and ongoing feedback.
üîü Human Needs - Maslow's Hierarchy
Maslow's needs hierarchy: A human needs theory proposes that people are motivated to satisfy unmet needs in a specific order.

The need hierarchy explains Maslow's conception of "people satisfying their needs in a specified order, from bottom to top":

  1. Physiological: food, water, and housing.
  2. Safety or security: protection against risk and lack.
  3. Social: friendship, relationships and love.
  4. Appreciation/Esteem: independence, achievement, freedom, recognition, and self-esteem.
  5. Self-actualization: achieving your full potential and receiving recognition from others for your abilities and efforts.
  • Two general levels of needs: lower-level needs must be satisfied before higher-level needs become important.
  • Managers must be aware of the importance of personal growth and self-actualization.
1Ô∏è‚É£1Ô∏è‚É£ Do Need Theories Apply Internationally?
  • Need theories provide a foundational understanding of human motivation that can be applied internationally. However, the successful implementation of these theories in global environments requires careful attention of cultural differences.
  • Managers must remember that need importance differs from country to country and that people may not be motivated by the same needs.

Cultural differences influence how people collective individual achievement.

In group-focused cultures like Japan, teamwork, family relationships, and shared celebrations hold great value.

On the other hand, self-focused cultures like the United States, personal initiative, individual achievements, independence, and personal successes.

Understanding and respecting these cultural differences is essential for effective communication and collaboration in different settings.

5Ô∏è‚É£ The 5 Stages of Group Development
  1. Forming: This stage is complete when members begin to think of themselves as part of a group.
  2. Storming: The storming stage is complete when members agree on the leadership hierarchy and group direction.
  3. Norming: is the third stage of group development by close relationships and connections.
  4. Performing: is the fourth stage in group development when the group is fully functional.
  5. Adjourning: is the final stage of group development for temporary groups, with a focus on completing activities rather than ongoing tasks.
3Ô∏è‚É£ Group vs. Team
  • Group: A collection of people who interact to start a task but do not necessarily perform as a unit or achieve significant performance improvements.
  • Team: A small number of people who are committed to a shared objective, a set of performance goals.
Aspect Groups Teams
Definition A collection of individuals who come together for a common purpose, interest, or activity. A specific type of group formed with a clear goal or objective in mind and works together to achieve it.
Purpose Groups may have a shared purpose, but individual members may work independently. Teams have a shared purpose, and members collaborate closely to achieve common goals.
Interaction Interaction may be limited, and members may not rely on each other to complete tasks. Interaction is essential, and they depend on each other to accomplish their objectives.
Roles and Structure Groups may have loosely defined roles, and leadership may not be clearly established. Teams often have defined roles and leadership structures, with designated leaders or coordinators.
Accountability Accountability for individual contributions may be lower in groups. Accountability is higher in teams, as members are responsible for their specific roles and outcomes.
Examples Social clubs (e.g., book clubs, hobby groups), Classroom students, Audience at a concert Sports teams (e.g., soccer, basketball), Project teams at work, Cross-functional teams in organizations
Performance Performance may not be a primary focus, and there may be no formal measures of success. Performance is a key focus, and success is often measured against specific goals or metrics.
Cohesion Cohesion can differ widely, and there may be limited trust. Teams typically strive for high cohesion, building trust and strong relationships among members.
Decision-Making Decision-making can be hierarchical or democratic, depending on the context. Teams often involve collaborative decision-making processes where input from all members is valued.
Communication Communication may be less frequent and structured in groups. Teams prioritize effective and frequent communication to ensure everyone is on the same page.
Flexibility and Adaptability Groups may be less adaptable to change or new tasks. Teams are often more adaptable and can adjust to new challenges or objectives as needed.

üìñ Week 14: Controlling

1Ô∏è‚É£ What Is Controlling, and Why Is It Important?
Control is the process of monitoring, comparing, and correcting work performance.

Control is important for three main reasons:

  • Control helps as the final link in the functional chain of management.
  • Controlling is also important to employee empowerment. The development of an effective control system may decrease organizational conflicts.
  • Finally, control assists to protect the company and its assets.
2Ô∏è‚É£ The Control Process

The control process consists of three steps:

  1. Measurement of the actual performance
  2. Comparison between planned activity and actual performance
  3. Taking managerial action
3Ô∏è‚É£ Step 1: Measurement of Actual Performance

Measurement of the actual performance is normally achieved through four common sources of information:

  • Personal observation
  • Statistical reports
  • Oral reports
  • Written reports

Examples:

  • Keeping costs within budget is also a common control measure.
  • Employee satisfaction or turnover and absenteeism rates can be measured.
  • A manager at a pizza delivery location might use measures such as the number of pizzas delivered per day, average delivery time, or the number of coupons redeemed.
  • A manager in a governmental agency might use typed applications daily, client requests completed per hour, or average time to process paperwork.
4Ô∏è‚É£ What We Measure? Why?
  • A manager must first get information about the control criteria to determine actual performance.
  • Selecting the wrong criteria can create serious problems.
  • What is measured often controls what employees will do.
5Ô∏è‚É£ Step 2: Comparison

This step involves comparing the actual performance measured in Step 1 against the planned standards or goals.

6Ô∏è‚É£ Step 3: Taking Managerial Action

Correct Actual Performance:

Once the manager has decided to correct actual performance, he/she must make another decision: corrective actions.

  • Correction is taking action to correct a problem. It's a direct action that corrects problems to get performance back on track.
  • Corrective is taking action to correct the Reason/cause of the problem and prevent it from happening again. look at how and why performance move away from the planned activity.

Example: Furniture shipped to a customer were damaged in transfer.

  • Correction Action: directly replace the damaged sets.
  • Corrective action: the reason of the damage needs to be addressed, take action to prevent it from happening again.
